
# The basics of Pandunia

Pandunia is a constructed language that has a minimalistic grammar and a globalistic vocabulary.

## Letters and sounds

Pandunia uses a systematic spelling where
each letter represents one spoken sound.

> A B C D E F G H I J K L M N O P R S T U V X Y Z

The vowels *a, e, i, o, u* are pronounced as in "are there three or two".

Consonants are pronounced in Pandunia as they are in English, with the following exceptions:

-   *c* is pronounced like *ch* in *church* but also like *c* in *center*.
-   *g* is always hard like in *get*.
-   *j* sounds like the English *j* in *jet*.
-   *v* sounds like *w* in *wet*.
-   *x* sounds like *x* in *box* or like *s* in *set*.

### What syllable to stress

Stress falls on the first syllable of the root,
which is the body of the word without prefixes and suffixes.
Prefixes and suffixes are usually unstressed.  
***hom*** 'person', ***su****pa* 'soup', ***po****pul* 'people', ***du****nia* 'world', *di****fe****ren* 'different'.

### Words

Words are made up of one or more roots and an ending.
The ending indicates the word-class or "part of speech".

-   Nouns end in *-e* or in a consonant
-   Adjectives end in *-i*
-   Adverbs end in *-o*
-   There are two endings for verbs:
    -   Verbs that end in *-a* indicate the subject–verb–object  (SVO) word order.
    -   Verbs that end in *u* indicate the subject–object–verb (SOV) word order.

### Nouns

Nouns indicate a group of one or more; definite or indefinite; masculine, neuter or feminine.
Number, definiteness and gender are indicated by additional words.

*mau*
– a cat, the cat, cats, the cats  
*un mau*
– one cat   
*multi mau*
– many cats  
*yi mau*
– the/this/that cat(s)

### Personal pronouns

| Person   | Singular          | Plural       |
|:---------|:-----------------:|:------------:|
|          | *me*              | *memen*      |
| 1st      | 'I, me'           | 'we, us'     |
|          | *tu*              | *tumen*      |
| 2nd      | 'you'             | 'you all'    |
|          | *ho*              | *homen*      |
| 3rd      | 'he/she, him/her' | 'they, them' |

### Basic sentence structures

There are two basic clause word orders in Pandunia.
One is subject–verb–object (SOV)
– the same as in English!
The other one is subject–object–verb (SVO),
which is used in Turkish, Hindi, Japanese and many other languages.
Verb endings help to distinguish the subject, verb and object.

*me ama tu.* (SVO)  
*me tu amu.* (SOV)  
'I love you.'


### Negation

To negate a word, add
*no*
before it.

*me no sona.*
– I don't sleep.  
*le yama no jen.*
– It eats no-one.

### To be

The verb
*sa*
means 'to be'.
It can be left out in very simple sentences.

*me sa hom.*
– I am a human.  
*me – hom.*
– I'm a human.

It can't be left out when it is part of a serial verb construction.

*me vola sa mau.*
– I want to be a cat.  
*me vola mau!*
– I want a cat!

### Multi-purpose words

Pandunia words can be ambiguous because they often have more meanings compared to English.

*ho ama mau.*
– He loves cats. ~ She loved a cat. ~ He or she will love the cat.

However, the precise meaning is usually clear in the actual context where the sentence is used.

*a pre da tri nien, me hava du mau e un gau. me hava maxo un dote. ho ama mau!*
– Three years ago, I had two cats and a dog. I also had a daughter. _She loved the cats!_

### Time

Tenses and aspects are optionally expressed with the help of adverbs and auxiliary verbs.

*me sta ama tu.*
– I am loving you.  
*me ha ama tu.*
– I have loved you.  
*me pasa ama tu.*
– I loved you.  
**me fu ama tu.**
– I will love you.

### Passive

The passive voice is formed by using the verb ending *-u*
and changing the word order to the opposite,
so that the patient of the action is before the verb and the agent is after the verb.

*tu amu.*
– You are loved.  
*tu amu me.*
– You are loved by me.

### Modifying other words

Adjectives end in
*-i*.
They are usually before the noun that they modify,
but they can be can be after the noun, too.

*novi ame*
– a new love  
*boni pai*
– a good pie

Adverbs end in
*-o*.
They can be placed freely, but usually they are before the word that they modify.

*me bono viza tu.*
– I see you well.  
*tu vizablo ama ho.*
– You seemingly love him/her.  
*me multo ama tu.*
– I greatly love you.  
*me ama tu multo.*
– I love you greatly.


Adjectives and adverbs are compared with
*max*
'more, -er',
*maxim*
'the most...',
*min*
'less',
*minim*
'the least...', and
*par*
'equally, as'.
The point of comparison is introduced with
*ka*
'than, as'.

*tu sa max juni ka me.*
– You are younger than me.  
*tu sa maxim juni.*
– You are the youngest.

### Saying no

The word
*no*
denies the word that follows it.

*no me a ho vola cepa ho*
– Not me but he/she wants to take it.  
*me no vola cepa ho.*
– I don't want to take it.  
*me vola no cepa ho.*
– I want _not_ to take it.  
*me vola cepa no ho a de.*
– I want to take not it but that.

### Questions

To ask _yes–or–no_ questions, replace the verb with "(verb) *no* (verb)" pattern or add
*he*
'eh, huh' to the end of the sentence.

*tu viza no viza me?*
– Do you see me?  
*tu viza me, he?*
– You see me, eh?

To ask a content question, write a normal sentence and replace the word in question with
**ke**
'what, who'.

*tu ama ke?*
– Who do you love?  
*tu ama ke man?*
– Which man do you love?  
*ke man ama tu?*
– Which man loves you?  
*tu ama ho ke multi?*
– How much do you love him?

### Commands

To state a command, leave out the subject and start the sentence with the verb.

*yama ye pai!*
– Eat this pie!  
*viza vo mau!*
– Look at that cat!
